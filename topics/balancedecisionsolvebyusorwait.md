You’re really asking about how to judge **when to act yourself vs. when to rely on better tools, systems, or other people**. That’s less about a single rule and more about building a balanced mental model.

A good way to think about it is to weigh three dimensions: **urgency, control, and leverage**.

If something is **urgent and directly affects you**, waiting for technology or others is usually a mistake. Even if your solution isn’t perfect, taking action prevents things from getting worse and gives you feedback. In these cases, “good enough now” beats “perfect later.”

If the issue is **important but not time-sensitive**, that’s where patience and leverage come in. Technology, better information, or collaboration can dramatically improve outcomes. Waiting can be smart—but only if you’re actively monitoring or preparing, not just avoiding the problem.

Control matters too. If you **have high control** over the situation (your habits, your work, your decisions), default toward handling it yourself. If you **have low control** (large systems, complex technical problems, policy-level issues), then trying to brute-force it alone is inefficient—this is where tools, experts, or collective solutions make more sense.

A simple internal check you can use:

* *If I do nothing, does this get worse?* → act now
* *Can I meaningfully influence this myself?* → lean toward acting
* *Would waiting significantly improve the outcome?* → consider waiting strategically

The trap on one side is **over-reliance on future solutions** (“technology will fix it”), which can become passive avoidance. The trap on the other side is **trying to solve everything yourself**, which wastes effort on problems better handled by systems or scale.

The balanced mindset is:
**Act where you have leverage and responsibility. Wait where timing and scale clearly favor better solutions—but do so intentionally, not passively.**
